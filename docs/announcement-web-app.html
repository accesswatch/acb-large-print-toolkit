The Problem: Documents Are Chaotic. Standards Are Complex.

Every ACB chapter produces documents: meeting agendas, newsletters, election ballots, board reports. Every one of those documents should be readable for people with low vision. But the formatting rules are dense. Is your font size right? Is your spacing correct? Did you accidentally use justified alignment? Should those be underlined instead of bold?

Most people just hope for the best. Some copy a template and pray they did not mess it up. A few volunteer editors spend hours learning the rules and applying them manually to every single document.

GLOW (Guided Layout & Output Workflow) changes that. You do not have to become an accessibility expert to make accessible documents. GLOW does the thinking for you.

What GLOW (Guided Layout & Output Workflow) Actually Does For You

Audit (Check Your Document)

Upload your Word, PDF, PowerPoint, Excel, Markdown, or ePub file. GLOW reads it like a human would and tells you what is right and what needs fixing.

The results appear in your browser organized by severity -- Critical (stop), High (fix soon), Medium (should fix), Low (nice to improve). Each finding explains what is wrong in plain English:

"Heading 2 is 16 point, but it should be 20 point for large print"

not

"DOCX-ACB-H1-001: Heading level 2 font size does not match standard"

You get an instant compliance score. You know where you stand.

Auto-Fix (Let GLOW Fix It)

Upload a Word document. GLOW fixes fonts, sizes, spacing, alignment, emphasis, margins -- everything that can be automatically corrected. Download a corrected version with one action. A before-and-after score shows you exactly how much it improved.

Want to control what gets fixed? GLOW lets you pick. Fix only the critical issues, or go the whole nine yards.

Standards Profiles (Now in Audit, Fix, and Template)

GLOW now includes profile selection so teams can keep existing operations stable while preparing APH submission evidence:

  • ACB 2025 Baseline (default): no impact to existing users. If you select ACB, behavior remains the same.
  • APH Submission (Current Coverage): focuses reports on APH-aligned checks that are implemented today.
  • Combined Strict: includes all currently implemented checks together for rigorous final QA.

Each report shows the selected profile so review conversations are clear and traceable.

Why we added this now:

The community asked for two things at the same time: keep existing ACB chapter workflows stable, and open a practical path for APH submission readiness without forcing everyone into a one-size-fits-all process. The profile model solves that tension.

Think about three real-world stories we kept hearing:

  1. A chapter newsletter team has a monthly deadline and cannot afford surprises. They stay on ACB 2025 Baseline so nothing changes operationally, but they can still run APH-focused checks when needed.
  2. A state affiliate preparing a formal review packet needs APH-aligned evidence now, not "someday." They select APH Submission (Current Coverage) to generate traceable reports based on implemented APH-aligned checks.
  3. A quality lead doing final sign-off before publication wants the strictest practical gate. They run Combined Strict to see all implemented checks together and catch issues before release.

This is about optionality with clarity: users can choose the right standards lens for their context, while preserving trust in the default ACB workflow.

Here is what that looks like in day-to-day work:

  • A volunteer editor opens GLOW at 8:45 AM before a 9:00 AM chapter meeting. They keep ACB baseline selected, run a fast audit, and publish on time with no workflow surprises.
  • A statewide communications lead spends an afternoon assembling a board packet. They switch to APH profile and export evidence-ready reports that match their review conversation.
  • A documentation owner preparing final release artifacts runs Combined Strict as a last gate, then locks that workflow in Settings so the same checks apply next cycle.

In other words, APH integration was not added to replace ACB. It was added to give organizations a practical bridge: continue producing accessible content today while gaining more options to align and report across multiple standards.

"We do not want users to pick between stability and progress," Bishop says. "The whole point is to give teams a safe default, a stronger APH path, and a strict final review option without making anyone relearn the product."

Template creation now also respects profile intent:

  • ACB keeps current template defaults.
  • APH profile applies APH-oriented template defaults.
  • Combined Strict keeps ACB template defaults while teams run strict combined review in Audit/Fix.

That template behavior was intentional. Teams told us they needed profile-aware options without destabilizing production templates. So APH-oriented template defaults are available when requested, while ACB-safe defaults remain predictable for everyday chapter publishing.

The Magic: Heading Detection

Here is where GLOW gets genuinely smart. You have a document with no built-in heading levels. Maybe it is a quick note scribbled into a Word doc. Maybe it is a PDF scanned from a printed document. Most tools give up.

GLOW learns your document. It looks at font sizes, bold formatting, positioning, spacing -- all the visual clues -- and says:

"This looks like a heading. This looks like a subheading. This is body text."

When the visual clues are clear, it just works. No human judgment needed.

When things get ambiguous (like a title that might or might not be a heading), GLOW can ask you: "Is this a heading?" You click yes or no, and GLOW learns and applies your answer to similar content in the document. You can also choose the heading level from an easy review interface.

And if even human intuition fails, GLOW can optionally use AI to learn your document's structure from the content, patterns, and context. It is not magic, it is machine learning -- but to users, it often feels magical.

Important note: This is not perfect. We will continue evolving the technology with community feedback and real-world usage.

(And if you are worried about privacy: GLOW does not send your document to third-party AI services. The AI option is local and self-hosted in our deployment model.)

Community Built for the Community: Washington Council of the Blind

Some of the most important document-experience changes came directly from real-world feedback shared by the Washington Council of the Blind. Their notes did not come from a lab. They came from production newsletters, 100-page workflows, and the practical constraints of mailing physical large-print copies.

That feedback was direct, thoughtful, and deeply operational:

  • "If faux heading detection is off, scoring should not keep penalizing us for faux headings."
  • "Some workflows require raw URLs for print and narrated audio use."
  • "Decorative images should not force unnecessary alt-text churn."
  • "Centering can be meaningful for titles and poems; do not flatten visual hierarchy by default."
  • "Long-document fixes can dramatically increase page count, which affects binding and mailing logistics."

Those insights shaped major document experience updates, including:

  • Better handling when heading detection is intentionally disabled, so scoring reflects user-selected workflow intent.
  • Quick rule exceptions for link text and missing alt text where policy and publishing workflows require targeted flexibility.
  • A preserve-centered-headings option for users who intentionally center specific heading/title structures.
  • New heading-level controls so teams can restrict heading conversion and review to a selected subset (for example, H1-H3 only).
  • Heading review that honors those allowed levels end to end, so the dropdown choices match the workflow a team actually uses.
  • Template generation that honors the same heading-level preferences for sample content, keeping creation and remediation workflows aligned.
  • Expanded FAQ and guidance to answer the real formatting questions teams face under deadline pressure.

"As a WCB newsletter editor, this felt like the first time the tool met us where we really work," said one Washington Council of the Blind editor. "Now when GLOW finds heading candidates, it only offers the levels we actually use, and our template follows the same structure. It is like the app already knows our publishing rhythm and quietly clears the path."

This is what community-driven engineering looks like in practice: members bring real document stories, and the product evolves to serve the people actually doing the work.
